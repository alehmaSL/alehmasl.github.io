Introduction

What do we mean when we say "Eating disorders"?

✮ Eating disorders are behavioral conditions characterized by severe and persistent disturbance in eating behaviors and associated distressing thoughts and emotions. They can be severe conditions affecting physical, psychological, and social function. Eating disorders can occur in individuals of any age from children to older adults. However, studies show that teenage girls and young women have the highest risk factor for developing eating disorders based on age.

Characteristics

Causes

There are a combination of biological, psychological, and sociocultural influences:

    ⋆ Genetic and Biological Factors→ These factors can include a family history of eating disorders, imbalances in brain chemicals that regulate mood and appetite, and hormonal irregularities.
    ⋆ Psychological Factors→ These can include low self-esteem, perfectionism, a negative body image, poor body image perception, difficulty expressing emotions, and a tendency towards obsessive-compulsive thinking patterns.
    ⋆ Sociocultural Factors→ Societal pressures to attain a particular body ideal, media portrayals of thinness, dieting culture, and the emphasis on appearance can contribute to body dissatisfaction and disordered eating behaviors.
    ⋆ Traumatic Experiences→ Individuals who have experienced traumatic events, such as physical, emotional, or sexual abuse, may be more vulnerable to developing eating disorders as a coping mechanism or an attempt to regain control over their bodies.
    ⋆ Family and Environmental Factors→ Dysfunctional family dynamics, enmeshment, neglect, high parental expectations, and critical attitudes toward weight and appearance.
    ⋆ Dieting and Weight-Related Factors→ Restrictive diets, frequently losing and gaining weight, or having a history of weight-related teasing or bullying.

Types of ED's

The most common ones are:

    𓇽Anorexia Nervosa→ Is characterized by an intense fear of gaining weight and a distorted body image. Individuals restrict their food intake, leading to significant weight loss, an obsession with food and calories, and a preoccupation with thinness. They may also engage in excessive exercise, use diuretics or laxatives, and experience the loss of menstrual periods.
    𓇽Bulimia Nervosa→ Binge eating followed by behaviors to avoid weight gain. Binge eating episodes are characterized by consuming a large amount of food within a discrete period, accompanied by a sense of loss of control. Compensatory behaviors can include self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives or diuretics, excessive exercise, or fasting. Individuals with bulimia often experience feelings of guilt, shame, and low self-esteem
    𓇽Binge Eating Disorder (BED)→ Individuals with BED experience a loss of control during these episodes and often eat large amounts of food quickly, even when not physically hungry.
    𓇽Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) → Avoidance or restriction of food intake based on the sensory characteristics of food, such as texture, color, smell, or taste. It is not associated with body image or weight concerns, but it can lead to significant nutritional deficiencies and weight loss.

Consequences

The specific consequences can vary depending on the type and severity of the eating disorder:

    * Physical Consequences→ Malnutrition, Electrolyte Imbalance, Gastrointestinal Problems, Hormonal Irregularities, Cardiovascular Issues, Osteoporosis, Dental Problems.
    * Psychological and Emotional Consequences→ Depression and Anxiety, Obsessive-Compulsive Behaviors, Body Image Disturbance, Social Withdrawal and Isolation.
    * Long-Term Health Consequences→ Chronic Health Issues, Increased Risk of Suicide, Co-occurring Disorders.
